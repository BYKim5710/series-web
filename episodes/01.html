Author Introduction

Byung Youn Kim
Byung Youn Kim — author of the “Conversation with Oneness God” series.

Born in 1957, Byung Youn Kim graduated from Yonsei University (Business Administration) and completed the MBA program at Purdue University. He worked for over 20 years at Daewoo Shipbuilding and Samsung Electronics, mainly in marketing, and later served as Head of Internationalization and Foreign Language Education at Samsung Human Resources Development Center.

He later taught as an adjunct professor/lecturer at multiple universities, and currently runs Dures Management Research Institute, a company he founded. His published works include The Samsung Myth Has Yet a Long Way to Go, The Gordian Knot, Opening Pandora’s Box of Korea, Thirty Letters to Jeong-Ah, and Soul and Zero (靈과 零).

A childhood encounter with death led him to lifelong inquiry. In 1977, he experienced a life-altering insight and wrote down a statement he still remembers:

“All beings consist of body, mind, and spirit. The body and mind, which experience fatigue, will perish. But the spirit, which has existed since the beginning and will exist eternally, is the essence of all beings. This spirit never dies and continues to exist.”

He later pursued answers through scriptures and philosophy, and came to believe that the laws of religion and society are not separate from nature, but closely interconnected—leading him toward a framework he calls “Divine Consilience.”

Book Introduction

Purpose · Structure · Key Themes

The title Conversation with Oneness God reflects the author’s view that the central teaching of both the Buddha and Jesus is that “all beings are one,” and that a true understanding of this naturally leads to a clearer understanding of the divine.

The subtitle, A Spiritual Solution through Divine Consilience, emphasizes the author’s method: seeking answers in the context of 21st-century advanced natural sciences, and integrating social and natural sciences beyond what he calls earlier “Old Consilience.”

Author’s intent

  • To help readers gain a clearer understanding of the afterlife and the meaning of God, and live accordingly.
  • To emphasize that the Holy Spirit is not exclusive to religious people; it dwells equally in all beings.
  • To call religious institutions that concealed or distorted truth to repentance and reorientation toward their true function.
  • To plant a “seed and spark” for a genuine Religious Revolution.

Structure of the Book (4 Chapters)

  • Chapter 1 — Analyzes contradictions in the Old Testament; argues much of it is mythology based on partial historical fact, and explains why Jesus proclaimed a new covenant distinct from the Old Testament.
  • Chapter 2 — Explains how Jesus’s revolutionary intentions for social reform were thwarted, and how Paul’s ideas became a foundation of modern Christian doctrine.
  • Chapter 3 — Exposes the fallacy of the afterlife as commonly taught and reexamines the meaning of “heaven on earth” preached by Jesus.
  • Chapter 4 — Connects truths declared by enlightened figures such as Jesus and the Buddha regarding God and the essence of existence with principles of natural law.

Recommendation

Jongrok Lee (PhD, Old Testament Studies)

Jongrok Lee, an Old Testament scholar and longtime seminary professor, frames his recommendation around a striking problem: many Christians—including pastors—do not read, study, or properly understand the Bible, relying instead on stereotypes.

“The people who are supposed to be Bible experts don’t know the Bible… the majority of Christians who hold it up as the absolute truth don’t bother to read it, don’t study it, don’t understand it properly… and reject what biblical scholars teach.”

He describes the author as a lifelong seeker after truth—concerned with Tao, life, death, and what lies beyond—who read the Bible more often and more thoroughly than many believers, pastors, and theologians, and who sought advice from experts to critique it seriously.

  • He calls the author “lovable and precious” not only as a longtime friend, but as a sincere truth-seeker.
  • He emphasizes the author’s extraordinary effort: extensive reading, many conversations, a huge manuscript, and countless revisions.
  • He recommends the book as a “warning to Christians” to recognize their laziness and ignorance about the Bible.
  • He hopes Christians will read it and awaken to how crude their stereotypes are—precisely because they do not read the text they claim to honor.

In closing, he dedicates this recommendation to his friend, “who has chosen to live the life of a hard truth seeker” in order to realize and share the universal Tao.
