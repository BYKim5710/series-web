Author Introduction

Byung Youn Kim
Byung Youn Kim — background in business administration, MBA studies, and long experience in marketing and education leadership. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Byung Youn Kim (born 1957) studied Business Administration at Yonsei University and completed an MBA program at Purdue University. He worked over 20 years in major Korean companies, primarily in marketing, and also led internationalization and foreign language education initiatives. Later, he taught as an adjunct professor/lecturer at multiple universities and currently runs a research institute he founded. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

A formative experience occurred in childhood when he confronted the fear of death; later, in 1977, he described a life-altering moment of insight. The note he remembers centered on the idea that while body and mind perish, spirit persists as “the essence of all beings.” :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

“All beings consist of body, mind, and spirit… the spirit… is the essence of all beings… This spirit never dies and continues to exist.” :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Book Introduction

Why “Oneness” · Why “Divine Consilience”

The title Conversation with Oneness God reflects the author’s view that the central teaching of both the Buddha and Jesus is that “all beings are one,” and that understanding this leads to a clearer understanding of the divine. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

The subtitle, A Spiritual Solution through Divine Consilience, points to a method: searching for answers in the context of modern natural sciences, and integrating insights across social and natural sciences—moving beyond what the author calls earlier, less precise “Old Consilience.” :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

Structure of the Book (4 Chapters)

  • Chapter 1: Contradictions in the Old Testament—why much of it should be read as mythology with partial historical bases, and why Jesus proclaimed a new covenant. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
  • Chapter 2: How Jesus’s social reform intentions were thwarted, and how Paul’s ideas shaped later doctrine. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
  • Chapter 3: Reexamining the afterlife and “heaven on earth.” :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}
  • Chapter 4: Connecting enlightenment claims about God and the essence of existence with principles of natural law. :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}

Recommendation

Jongrok Lee · Old Testament Scholar

The recommender is an Old Testament scholar who has taught in a seminary context for decades. He writes from the standpoint that many Christians—including pastors—do not read or seriously study the Bible, and he views that as a serious problem. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

He describes the author as someone who read the Bible more frequently and thoroughly than many believers, and who pursued religious questions about Tao, life, death, and what lies beyond, even without adopting traditional theistic belief. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

The recommendation emphasizes that this book can serve as a warning to Christians: those who claim the Bible as absolute truth often do not read it, do not understand it properly, and cling to stereotypes—while rejecting what scholars teach. :contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}

Key Points Highlighted in the Recommendation

  • The author’s effort is portrayed as extraordinary—reading widely, consulting experts, revising repeatedly, and building a comprehensive critique. :contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}
  • The book is recommended not because it flatters Christianity, but because it challenges complacency and “Bible-ignorance” within Christianity. :contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}
  • It calls readers to face hard questions rather than hide behind authority, habit, or inherited belief. :contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}
